How to Receive

How Should I Approach Holy Communion?

Receiving the Eucharist is the center of Catholic life, but it's also surrounded by customs and expectations that can feel confusing, especially if you're new, returning after time away, or just visiting.

1

Who Can Receive?

Catholics

  • Baptized Catholic. To receive Holy Communion, a person must be a baptized Catholic who has made their First Communion — whether that happened in childhood or as an adult through OCIA.
  • State of grace. Catholics are asked to receive only when not conscious of having committed a serious (mortal) sin that hasn't yet been confessed. If that's the case, go to Confession before receiving Communion again (see "Returning to the Sacraments" below).

The Eucharistic fast: Catholics are asked to fast from food and drink (water and medicine excepted) for at least one hour before receiving Communion as a small physical reminder that this is not ordinary food.

Non-Catholics

Catholics believe that receiving Communion together is a sign of full, visible unity — not only about the Eucharist itself, but also about the unity of Catholic belief, and about being in communion with one's bishop and the Pope.

For this reason, the Church asks those who are not Catholic — including members of other Christian traditions — not to receive Communion at Mass. This isn't a judgment on anyone's faith or sincerity. It's an honest acknowledgment that the sign of unity that Communion expresses isn't yet fully true between us.

Visitors who don't receive are warmly welcome to be present, to pray, and to make a "spiritual communion" (see Encounter Jesus and "If You Cannot Receive" below).

If you're interested in becoming Catholic, find a Catholic parish near you and speak with the priest about their OCIA program.

2

Returning to the Sacraments

For Catholics who have been away — whether for months, years, or decades.

If it's been a while, an examination of conscience can help you prepare — most parishes post regular confession times, and most priests are glad to help you through it if it's been a while.

You Will Be Welcomed, Not Interrogated

If it has been months, years, or decades since your last confession or Communion, you are not the first person to walk back through that door, and you will not be the last. The same Christ who is present in the Eucharist is the one waiting for you — not to shame you, but to welcome you home.

3

Receiving Reverently

The Church permits several legitimate ways of receiving Holy Communion, and you may notice different customs depending on where you are.

On the Tongue

The communicant opens their mouth and the priest or minister places the host directly on the tongue. This remains the universal norm in the Church's law, though Communion in the hand is now widely permitted as well.

Kneeling

In some places the faithful kneel at an altar rail to receive — a posture with a long history, expressing adoration of the One being received.

Standing

In many places the faithful stand to receive, often making a sign of reverence — such as a bow of the head — just beforehand.

In the Hand

Where permitted, the communicant forms a small "throne" with one hand beneath the other, the minister places the host in the palm, and the communicant consumes it immediately, in view of the minister.

Different customs exist, but all should be marked by reverence for Our Lord. However you receive, the goal is the same: to approach with the awareness that you are receiving God himself.

4

If You Cannot Receive

Spiritual Communion

If you can't receive Communion — whether you're not Catholic, not yet able to receive, separated from the sacraments, or unable to attend Mass — you can make a "spiritual communion": an act of faith and longing, asking Jesus to come into your soul even though you can't receive him sacramentally. See the Encounter Jesus page for a traditional prayer.

Remaining in Prayer

During the Communion procession, you can simply stay in your pew and pray — there's no need to feel awkward or out of place. Use the time to pray, to long for the day you'll be able to receive, or to make a spiritual communion.

Crossed-Arms Blessing

In some parishes, those who don't receive Communion — including children who haven't yet made their First Communion — may come forward with arms crossed over their chest to receive a blessing from the priest instead. This custom varies, so it's worth checking with your local parish.

5

Thanksgiving After Communion

The minutes after receiving Communion are uniquely intimate — Christ is present within you in a way unlike any other moment. Rather than rushing out, many Catholics use this time for quiet thanksgiving.

Say Thank You

In your own words, thank Jesus for coming to you. There's no formula required — simple, honest words are enough.

Ask

Bring whatever is on your heart — for yourself, or for someone you love — to the One who is, for these few minutes, as close to you as he can possibly be.

Be Still

Sit in silence. You don't need to fill the time with words. Presence doesn't require performance.
