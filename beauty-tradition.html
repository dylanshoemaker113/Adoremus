Eucharistic Hymns

Several of the Church's most enduring Eucharistic hymns were composed by St. Thomas Aquinas for the feast of Corpus Christi in the 13th century, and are still sung today.

Pange Lingua

Composed by St. Thomas Aquinas for Corpus Christi, this hymn meditates on the institution of the Eucharist at the Last Supper. Its final two verses are traditionally sung separately as the Tantum Ergo.

Tantum Ergo

The closing verses of Pange Lingua, sung especially during Eucharistic Benediction as the Blessed Sacrament is incensed and the blessing is given.

Adoro Te Devote

Also attributed to Aquinas, this hymn is a personal meditation on the Real Presence — an act of devotion addressed directly to the hidden God present in the Eucharist.

Panis Angelicus

Originally part of Aquinas's hymn Sacris Solemniis, this verse became famous on its own, especially after being set to music by composer César Franck in the 19th century.

Sacred Architecture

The way a church is built communicates belief, often before a single word is spoken.

Tabernacle — add image

Tabernacles

The locked container, usually on or near the altar, where the Eucharist is reserved between Masses. Designs vary across centuries and cultures, but the purpose is constant: a fitting "house" for the Real Presence.

Altar — add image

Altars

The altar is both a table and a place of sacrifice — the table at which the Eucharist is offered and from which it is distributed, echoing the altars of the Old Testament.

Baldachin — add image

Baldachins

A baldachin (or ciborium) is a canopy, often raised on columns, placed over an altar or tabernacle — architecture that signals something important happens here, the way a canopy over a throne signals royalty.

Sanctuary Lamp — add image

Sanctuary Lamps

A small lamp, traditionally fueled by oil or a candle, burns continuously near the tabernacle whenever the Eucharist is reserved — a quiet, constant sign that someone is home.

Why Beauty Matters

Beauty is not decoration laid on top of belief — it is itself a kind of witness. Long before someone can follow an argument about substance and accidents, they can sense, in a church filled with gold, incense, music, and silence, that something serious is happening here. Countless people have come to faith, or returned to it, not because an argument convinced them, but because something beautiful moved them to ask what it meant.

The Catholic tradition has never treated beauty as optional, or as a distraction from "real" faith. The art, architecture, music, and poetry gathered on this page exist because people believed Christ himself was present in their churches — and built and wrote accordingly. If that belief is true, the beauty isn't an exaggeration. If anything, it's an understatement.
