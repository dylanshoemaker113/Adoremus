Church Fathers & Saints

Received, Defended, and Lived

Belief in the Real Presence didn't end with Scripture. From the first generation after the apostles onward, the Church has received, defended, and lived this teaching — in writing, and in the lives of the saints.

The Early Church Saints & the Eucharist

Witnesses From

The Early Church

From the first generation after the apostles onward, Christian writers describe the Eucharist in the same terms: not as a symbol, but as the true Body and Blood of Christ.

c. 35–107 AD

St. Ignatius of Antioch

Bishop of Antioch and a disciple of the apostles, Ignatius wrote a series of letters on his way to martyrdom in Rome.

“Take note of those who hold heterodox opinions... They abstain from the Eucharist and from prayer, because they do not confess that the Eucharist is the flesh of our Savior Jesus Christ.” — Letter to the Smyrnaeans, 6:2–7:1
“...breaking one and the same bread, which is the medicine of immortality, and the antidote to prevent us from dying, but that we should live for ever in Jesus Christ.” — Letter to the Ephesians, 20:2
c. 100–165 AD

St. Justin Martyr

A philosopher who converted to Christianity and was later martyred in Rome, Justin wrote a defense of Christian worship addressed to the Roman emperor.

“Not as common bread and common drink do we receive these; but... the food which is blessed by the prayer of His word, and from which our blood and flesh by transmutation are nourished, is the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh.” — First Apology, 66
c. 130–202 AD

St. Irenaeus of Lyons

Bishop of Lyons, Irenaeus wrote extensively against Gnostic teachings that denied the goodness of the material world.

“The bread, which is produced from the earth, when it receives the invocation of God, is no longer common bread, but the Eucharist, consisting of two realities, earthly and heavenly.” — Against Heresies, IV.18.5
c. 313–386 AD

St. Cyril of Jerusalem

Bishop of Jerusalem, Cyril instructed new converts preparing for baptism in a series of lectures.

“Consider therefore the Bread and the Wine not as bare elements, for they are, according to the Lord's declaration, the Body and Blood of Christ; for even though sense suggests this to you, yet let faith establish you. Judge not the matter from the taste, but from faith be fully assured.” — Catechetical Lecture 22, 6
c. 347–407 AD

St. John Chrysostom

Archbishop of Constantinople and one of the greatest preachers in Christian history.

“It is not man that causes the things offered to become the Body and Blood of Christ, but he who was crucified for us, Christ himself.” — De proditione Judae, 1:6
“For what is the bread? The Body of Christ. And what do they become who partake of it? The Body of Christ.” — Homilies on 1 Corinthians, 24
354–430 AD

St. Augustine of Hippo

Bishop of Hippo in North Africa and one of the most influential theologians in Christian history.

“If, then, you are the body of Christ and His members, your mystery has been placed on the Lord's table... Be what you see, and receive what you are.” — Sermon 272
1225–1274 AD

St. Thomas Aquinas

Dominican friar and theologian whose work gave the Church its classical vocabulary — including the term "transubstantiation" — for explaining the Eucharist.

“It is the law of friendship that friends should live together... Christ has not left us without his bodily presence in this our pilgrimage, but he joins us to himself in this sacrament in the reality of his body and blood.” — Summa Theologiae, III, q. 75, a. 1

At the request of the Pope, he also composed several of the Church's most enduring Eucharistic hymns for the feast of Corpus Christi, still sung today.

Saints and the Eucharist

Doctrine is one way the Church teaches. Lives are another. These saints lived — and in some cases died — out of love for Christ in the Eucharist.

3rd Century

St. Tarcisius

A young acolyte in Rome during a time of persecution, entrusted with carrying the Eucharist to Christians in prison. Tradition holds that he was attacked by a mob who suspected he carried something valuable, and died rather than let them seize and desecrate the Blessed Sacrament. He is a patron of altar servers.

1194–1253

St. Clare of Assisi

Foundress of the Poor Clares. During a military siege of Assisi, Clare carried the Blessed Sacrament to the convent gate and prayed before it; tradition holds the attacking soldiers were turned back. Her life reflects deep confidence in the protective presence of Christ in the Eucharist.

1811–1868

St. Peter Julian Eymard

A French priest sometimes called "the Apostle of the Eucharist," he founded religious congregations dedicated entirely to perpetual Eucharistic adoration. He taught that devotion to the Eucharist was not one devotion among many, but the center from which every other virtue should grow.

1991–2006

Blessed Carlo Acutis

An Italian teenager known for an ordinary life — video games, soccer, computers — combined with extraordinary Eucharistic devotion. He attended daily Mass and adoration whenever possible, calling the Eucharist his "highway to heaven." He died of leukemia at fifteen and was beatified in 2020.
