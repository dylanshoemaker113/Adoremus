Eucharistic Miracles

When the Veil Is Drawn Back

Eucharistic miracles are extraordinary signs that the Church believes God has occasionally permitted. In most of these events, it is not only the substance that changes — as Catholics believe happens at every Mass — but the appearance as well: ordinary bread and wine become, visibly, flesh and blood. These events strengthen faith, but they are not its foundation. Catholic belief in the Real Presence rests on the words of Christ and the constant witness of the Church, with or without a miracle.

The Eucharistic Miracle of Lanciano

According to a tradition dating to the eighth century, a monk who doubted the Real Presence saw the host turn to visible flesh and the wine turn to blood during Mass. The relics have been venerated and displayed ever since.

Historical Context

Tradition holds that around the year 700 AD, a monk of the Basilian order was celebrating Mass in a small chapel near Lanciano, in the Abruzzo region of Italy, and was troubled by doubts about whether the bread and wine truly became the Body and Blood of Christ. At the moment of consecration, witnesses said the host visibly changed into a circle of flesh, and the wine in the chalice turned to blood, which then coagulated into five globules of unequal size and shape.

Timeline

  • c. 700 AD — The miracle is said to occur during Mass in a chapel outside Lanciano.
  • 1258 — The relics are moved to the Church of San Francesco in Lanciano for safekeeping, where they remain today.
  • 1574 — The Archbishop of Chieti conducts a formal ecclesiastical inventory, recording the size, weight, and appearance of the relics.
  • 1637 — A further inspection confirms the relics are unchanged.
  • 1970–71 — A scientific commission, led by Professor Edoardo Linoli, examines the relics using modern histological methods.
  • 1981 — A follow-up study reaffirms the earlier findings.
  • Today — The relics remain enshrined in the Sanctuary of the Eucharistic Miracle in Lanciano, visited by pilgrims year-round.

Church Investigation

The relics have been examined by Church authorities multiple times over the centuries, beginning with the formal 1574 inventory. Each subsequent examination found the relics essentially unchanged from previous records — notable, given that ordinary human tissue and blood would decompose within a short time.

Scientific Findings

In 1970–71, an interdisciplinary scientific commission examined the relics with modern laboratory techniques. According to the commission's report, the flesh was identified as human cardiac tissue, complete with structures such as the endocardium and a branch of the coronary artery, and the five globules were confirmed to be real human blood, type AB. The commission reported finding no trace of any preservatives or substances that would explain the relics' condition after so many centuries.

Church Approval Status

The Eucharistic Miracle of Lanciano has been venerated for over a thousand years with the long-standing support of local Church authorities, and the relics have been visited and venerated by several popes. It remains one of the most widely venerated Eucharistic miracles in the Catholic world.

The Eucharistic Miracle of Buenos Aires

In 1996, a consecrated host placed in water (the usual practice for a damaged host) was reported to develop the appearance of blood and living tissue over several days.

The Eucharistic Miracle of Sokółka

In 2008, a dropped host placed in water developed a red stain that, on examination by forensic pathologists, was reported to resemble fragments of a living human heart under great strain.
